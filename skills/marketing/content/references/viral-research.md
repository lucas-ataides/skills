# Viral research — research, adapt, test

Virality is not luck applied to a random post. A spike is a recognizable *pattern* — a
hook shape, a format, an emotional driver, and a save-or-share trigger — that happens to
land in a hungry feed at the right hour. The pattern is portable; the topic that carried
it this week is not. So the unit of work is the **transferable structure**: what made a
stranger stop, stay, and pass it on, stripped of the specific subject, then rebuilt in
the founder's niche and voice.

This reference is the engine room for live trend research. The durable strategy it
feeds — pillars, the hook-retain-reward spine, cadence, the give-before-you-sell ratio —
lives in [the content-system reference](content-system.md), and the hook-and-angle
taxonomy this borrows from lives in [the ad-angles reference](ad-angles.md). Trend
research is fuel for that engine, never a replacement for it: a founder who chases trends
without pillars buys reach that converts no one.

One rule overrides everything below. **Posting and every engagement action — like,
follow, reply, repost, schedule, DM — are external mutations that change the world.**
This job drives the browser to *observe and read only*. The moment an action would write
to a platform, stop and get the user's explicit approval. Research and drafting are the
whole job here.

## Decision procedure

1. **Frame the niche and the goal**: niche, target buyer, product, and the one metric
   this round chases (reach, saves, follows, or clicks).
2. **Scan the live surfaces by hand** in the user's logged-in browser at human pace, and
   capture 8 to 12 spiking posts with all seven fields.
3. **Extract each capture to its transferable pattern** using the taxonomy — name the
   pattern, not the surface topic.
4. **Score each pattern for fit** against the niche, the goal metric, and earned
   authority; drop the ones that fail.
5. **Adapt one surviving pattern** into the founder's niche and voice, keeping the
   skeleton and replacing every organ.
6. **Draft testable variations** with a primary hook and one alternate hook per draft,
   all labeled unposted.
7. **Lay out the post-and-test plan** on top of the baseline calendar, with a success
   signal and a double-down threshold per draft, left pending approval.

## The research method — manual browser scanning

The Chrome integration drives the user's already-logged-in Instagram and X. The browser
is the instrument because trend velocity is invisible from the outside: a topic that is
spiking *right now* shows up in the live For You feed and the Explore grid hours before
any trend report names it. Drive the session by hand, at human reading pace, the way the
user would scan their own feed. The goal is a small, well-observed sample, not a scrape.

Scan the live surfaces and capture what each rewards:

- **X — Trending.** Read the Trending panel and the per-topic pages for spikes adjacent
  to the niche. A trend with a *why now* attached (an event, a launch, a fight) is fuel;
  a trend with no angle into the niche is noise. Capture the topic and the angle that
  connects it to the founder's world.
- **X — niche search.** Search the niche's core terms, then sort by Top and by Latest.
  Top shows what already won; Latest shows what is accelerating. The gap between them is
  where an early-mover post can ride a rising wave before saturation.
- **X — For You.** Scroll the algorithmic feed and watch which post *shapes* recur — the
  one-line hot take, the numbered thread, the screenshot-plus-reaction. Recurrence is the
  signal; a shape the algorithm is amplifying this week is the shape to study.
- **Instagram — Explore, Reels, niche hashtags.** Open Explore, scroll the Reels tab,
  and open two or three niche hashtag pages. Note the cover frames that earn the tap, the
  audio attached to spiking Reels, and the carousel covers that promise a payoff worth a
  swipe.
- **TikTok — For You, Discover, trending sounds.** Scroll the For You feed for the
  short-video shapes the algorithm is pushing now, open Discover for the trending hashtags,
  and note the **sound** attached to each spiking video — a rising sound is itself a trend to
  ride. Capture the first-second hook, the format, and the sound.

For the 8 to 12 posts the procedure captures, record seven fields each, because a
pattern is only transferable once it is broken into parts:

| Field | What to capture | Why it matters |
|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| Platform | X, Instagram, or TikTok, and the surface (For You, Explore, Reels, Discover, hashtag) | Norms and formats differ per surface |
| Format | Standalone post, thread, carousel, Reel, short video | The format is half the pattern |
| Hook | The literal first line, first frame, or cover slide | The hook is the part most worth stealing |
| Structure | The beat order: hook, body shape, close | The skeleton the niche version reuses |
| Length | Word count, slide count, or video seconds | Length is a format constraint to match |
| Posting time | Approximate hour and day, in the account's timezone | Timing is a testable variable, not a given |
| Engagement signal | Likes, reposts, comments, saves/shares where visible; the ratio that stands out | Saves and shares predict reach better than likes |

A capture set is complete when every row carries all seven fields. A post recorded as
"this thread did well" is not a capture — the parts are missing, so nothing is
transferable.

## The viral-pattern taxonomy

A captured post reduces to four dimensions. Name the value on each, and the pattern
becomes a recipe a different topic can follow.

### Hook types

The hook's only job is to stop the scroll in the first line or first frame. The recurring
shapes, drawn from and consistent with [the ad-angles hook patterns](ad-angles.md):

- **Bold claim** — a specific, almost-too-strong promise tied to a number ("I grew to
  10k followers in 30 days with one format").
- **Contrarian** — attacks a belief the niche holds, then resolves it ("Posting daily is
  killing your account. Here's what I did instead.").
- **Curiosity gap** — withholds the payoff the reader must stay to get ("The one setting
  that doubled my reach — nobody talks about it").
- **Direct call-out** — names the audience so the right reader self-selects ("Founders
  with under 1k followers: read this").
- **Result-first** — opens on the outcome, then earns it ("$0 to first 100 customers from
  one thread").
- **Story cold-open** — drops the reader mid-scene with no setup, so the gap pulls them in.
- **Question hook** — asks the exact question the reader is already silently asking.
- **Listicle promise** — names a counted payoff up front ("7 hooks that went viral this
  month").

### Formats

The format is the container, and the container is platform-bound:

- **X standalone post** — one sharp idea or hot take; the unit of X, where volume and
  reply-velocity compound.
- **X thread** — a framework, a story, or a teardown; the hook tweet carries the open,
  each tweet is its own retain beat, the last tweet rewards and asks.
- **Instagram carousel** — the highest save-rate format on Instagram; the cover slide is
  the hook, each swipe advances one beat, the last slide is the reward and the call to
  action.
- **Instagram Reel / short video** — the widest reach to non-followers; the first second
  is the entire hook, captions are on, the frame is vertical.

### Emotional drivers

A post travels on emotion, not information. The dominant driver is the engine of the
share:

- **Aspiration** — the reader wants the end-state shown ("this could be me").
- **Relief / validation** — the post names a pain or a frustration the reader carries
  ("finally someone said it").
- **Outrage / disagreement** — the post sorts the audience into camps; the argument in
  the replies is the distribution.
- **Surprise** — the post breaks an expected pattern, and the break demands a second look.
- **Identity** — the post lets the reader signal who they are by sharing it ("this is so
  me").
- **Utility** — the post is useful enough to keep; usefulness is the quietest driver and
  the strongest for saves.

### Save-and-share triggers

Saves and shares move organic reach far more than likes, so the trigger is the part doing
the distribution work. The recurring triggers:

- **Reference value** — a checklist, a framework, or a swipe file the reader saves to use
  later.
- **Identity signal** — content the reader shares to say something about themselves.
- **Argument bait** — a take strong enough that the reader shares it to agree loudly or
  to dunk on it.
- **Practical utility** — a how-to concrete enough to act on, saved as a bookmark.
- **Emotional resonance** — a line so on-the-nose the reader sends it to one specific
  person.

The three lever families — **controversy**, **contrast**, and **utility** — map onto
these triggers. Controversy fires argument bait and outrage. Contrast (before/after,
expectation/reality, us/them) fires surprise and identity. Utility fires reference value
and practical saves. A pattern usually leads with one family; naming which one tells the
adaptation step what to preserve.

## Adapting a pattern authentically — steal the structure, not the content

The cardinal move: **keep the skeleton, replace every organ.** The hook *shape*, the
format, the beat order, and the emotional driver carry over. The topic, the claim, the
examples, the voice are rebuilt from the founder's own expertise and the buyer's own
pain. A post that copies the content is plagiarism that the audience smells; a post that
reuses the structure is craft.

Authenticity is the constraint that makes the adaptation convert rather than merely
spike. Run each candidate through three filters, the same intersection the
[content-system pillars](content-system.md) demand:

- **Earned authority.** The founder can speak to this with real credibility, not borrowed
  takes. A pattern about a domain the founder has never lived reads as costume, and the
  audience grown on it follows the costume, not the founder.
- **Buyer pain.** The adapted topic sits on a problem the target buyer already feels and
  already searches for. A trend with no line into the buyer's pain buys reach that the
  product can never convert.
- **Product adjacency.** The topic makes the eventual offer feel inevitable rather than
  bolted on. The post earns the sale long before the sale is mentioned.

A candidate that clears one filter and fails another gets cut or reshaped. Voice matters
as much as topic: the adapted draft has to sound like the founder talking, not like the
original author ventriloquized. Read the draft aloud — a line the founder would never say
is a line to rewrite.

## Platform-specific norms — X, Instagram, and TikTok

The same pattern is built differently per platform, because each room rewards a different
shape. Cross-posting identical text signals that the founder does not respect the room.

**X.** Text is the medium. Hooks live in the first line of the post copy itself, and that
copy is the creative, not a caption on it. The standalone post rewards a single sharp
idea; the thread rewards a framework with the hook tweet carrying the open. Reply-velocity
is half of X growth — the conversation under a post is part of its reach. Brevity wins; a
post that needs a paragraph of throat-clearing before the point dies on the timeline.
Links are tolerated but often suppressed, so the value lives in the post, with any link as
a reply or an afterthought.

**Instagram.** Visual-first. The cover frame of a Reel and the cover slide of a carousel
*are* the hook — a weak cover frame means the strong content is never seen. The carousel
is the save engine: a teaching framework or a checklist across slides, cover-slide hook,
last-slide reward and call to action. The Reel is the reach engine: the first second is
the whole hook, captions are on for the sound-off scroller, the aspect ratio is vertical.
Audio is a discovery surface on Reels — a spiking sound is itself a trend to ride. The
feed caption supports the visual; it does not carry the post the way X copy does.

**TikTok.** Sound-first and native-first. The first second is the whole hook, like a Reel,
but the bar for polish is lower and the bar for authenticity is higher — a clip that looks
like an ad dies. The **trending sound** is its own discovery surface: riding a rising sound
puts a video in front of everyone else using it. Text-on-screen carries the hook for the
sound-off scroller, and the For You algorithm rewards watch-through and rewatches over
follows, so the open and the loop matter more than the brand. A format that works on Reels
usually crosses to TikTok, rebuilt looser and faster.

The translation rule: an X thread's argument becomes an Instagram carousel's slides; an X
standalone's single sharp line becomes a Reel's spoken hook; the *idea* is shared across
rooms, the *execution* is rebuilt for each. A pattern observed on one platform can cross
to the other — the structure travels, the build does not.

## The daily radar — fetch, rank, report

The scan above is the deep method; the **daily radar** runs that same scan as a routine that
lands a ranked report each morning. The browser still does the reading — a scheduled session
drives the user's logged-in X, Instagram, and TikTok through the surfaces above at human pace
and records what it saw — but the ranking and the report are a script, so the output has the
same shape every day.

1. **Record the scan.** As the session reads each surface, write what it observed into a
   captures file:

   ```json
   {"niche": "<niche>", "captured_at": "<YYYY-MM-DD>",
    "trends": [{"platform": "x|instagram|tiktok", "topic": "...", "why_now": "...", "angle": "..."}],
    "posts":  [{"platform": "...", "surface": "...", "format": "...", "hook": "...",
                "structure": "...", "saves": 0, "shares": 0, "reposts": 0, "comments": 0, "likes": 0}]}
   ```

2. **Rank and render.** `python scripts/trends.py report captures.json` scores every captured
   post by the save-share-repost signal — likes never override it — and renders the radar:
   trends grouped by platform, the top patterns ranked, and the candidates to adapt. Same
   input, same report.

3. **Schedule it locally.** Run the routine daily with `cron` or `launchd` calling
   `claude -p "run the content daily radar"`, so the radar is waiting each morning; save it to
   a file or the second brain to see which patterns recur. The routine **reads only** — it
   observes the trend surfaces like a human checking trends over coffee, takes no action, and
   posts nothing.

The line the ethics section draws holds here: a once-a-day human-paced read is research, and
it never becomes bulk scraping or an automated action against the live account.

## The post-and-test loop

A trend-adapted post is a hypothesis, and a hypothesis is tested, not trusted. The loop
is cadence, variation, measurement, double-down.

- **Cadence.** Slot trend posts *on top of* the founder's baseline calendar from the
  content system, never in place of it. The baseline is what keeps the account alive on
  the weeks no trend lands; the trend post is the opportunistic bonus. One to two
  trend-driven posts a week is plenty for a solo founder — more dilutes the identity the
  pillars built.
- **Variation.** Test one variable at a time. Write two hooks for the same body and post
  them as a deliberate A/B across days, or run the same idea as a carousel on Instagram
  and a thread on X to read which format the idea fits. A test that changes the hook *and*
  the format *and* the time reads no clean signal.
- **Measurement.** Pick the signal that matches the goal: saves and shares for reach and
  durability, replies for conversation, profile visits and link clicks for conversion
  intent. Likes are the vanity metric — a high-like, low-save post is entertainment that
  will not compound. Read the signal against the goal metric named in step 1, not against
  raw applause.
- **Double-down.** A pattern that clears its threshold gets reused, not retired. Rerun the
  winning structure with a fresh topic, push it to the other platform, and turn it into a
  repeatable format the account is known for. The point of finding a winner is to run it
  again, which is how a single observed trend becomes a durable format.

## The ethics and ToS line

This line is not optional, and crossing it risks the user's accounts. Stay inside it.

- **Manual browsing only.** Drive the browser to read and observe at human pace, the way
  the user scans their own feed. Reading is the permitted operation. A scheduled daily pass
  over the trend surfaces is still reading — one human-paced scan, not a bulk harvest.
- **No scraping at scale.** Capturing a handful of posts by hand for study is research.
  Bulk-harvesting posts, profiles, or follower lists with automation violates both
  platforms' terms and is out of scope here.
- **No engagement farming.** Follow-for-follow, engagement pods, comment rings, and
  reply-bait loops manufacture hollow numbers and risk account action. Reach is earned by
  the post, not gamed by the loop.
- **No buying engagement.** Bought followers, likes, and views are fraud against the
  founder's own funnel — they inflate vanity metrics and depress the ratios the algorithm
  actually scores. Never buy, never recommend buying.
- **No covert automation of actions.** Automated posting, liking, following, or DMing
  against a logged-in human account violates platform terms and can get the account
  suspended. Posting and engagement are mutations that require the user's explicit
  approval, performed deliberately.

The principle under all five: observe like a human, act only with consent, never
manufacture what should be earned.

## Failure modes

Four patterns turn trend research into wasted effort or active harm. Name and check each
before drafting is called done.

- **Chasing trends off-brand.** The founder rides a spiking trend that has no line into
  the niche, the buyer's pain, or the product. The post may spike, but it grows an
  audience that came for the trend and leaves when the next one starts. Reach without
  relevance is a rented crowd. Fix: cut any pattern that fails the earned-authority or
  buyer-pain filter, however hot the trend.
- **Copying instead of adapting.** The draft lifts the original's wording, examples, or
  structure-and-content wholesale. The audience recognizes the theft, and the founder
  reads as derivative rather than authoritative. Fix: keep the skeleton, replace every
  organ, and rewrite in the founder's own voice until the original is unrecognizable in
  the words.
- **Vanity virality that does not convert.** The post optimizes for likes and views with
  no save trigger, no buyer relevance, and no path toward the offer. The numbers look
  great and the funnel stays empty. Fix: require a save-or-share trigger tied to buyer
  value on every draft, and measure against the goal metric, not against applause.
- **ToS-risky automation.** The plan reaches for bulk scraping, auto-posting,
  auto-engagement, or bought metrics to move faster. The account's standing is the price,
  and the standing is worth more than any single trend. Fix: hold the line above —
  manual browsing to research, explicit approval to act, nothing automated against the
  live account.

## Red flags

Stop and rework the plan the moment one of these appears:

- A captured post recorded without its hook, format, or engagement signal — the pattern
  is not transferable yet.
- An adapted draft whose topic the founder cannot speak to with earned credibility — the
  costume problem.
- A draft riding a trend with no line into the buyer's pain or the product — reach that
  will not convert.
- A draft that reuses the original's content, not just its structure — the copy problem.
- A plan that measures success in likes alone, with no save, share, reply, or click
  signal named — the vanity trap.
- Any step that proposes scraping at scale, buying engagement, engagement farming, or
  automating a post or an action against the live account — the ToS line crossed.
- A trend post that replaces the baseline calendar rather than sitting on top of it — the
  system traded for a lottery ticket.
- A draft presented as posted, or an engagement action taken, without the user's explicit
  approval — the consent line crossed.

## Worked example — one observed pattern, three niche post ideas

**The founder.** Sells a $49/month analytics tool for indie e-commerce store owners.
Niche: solo and small-team Shopify operators. Buyer pain: flying blind on which products
and channels actually make money. Goal this round: saves (durable reach) plus profile
visits (conversion intent).

**The observed pattern (captured on X, For You feed).**

| Field | Captured value |
|-------|----------------|
| Platform | X — For You |
| Format | Thread |
| Hook | "I audited 50 [creators'] accounts. Here are the 7 mistakes 90% make:" |
| Structure | Bold-claim hook tweet, then one numbered mistake per tweet each with a one-line fix, then a last tweet: free checklist + soft CTA |
| Length | 8 tweets, ~30 words each |
| Posting time | Tuesday, ~9am ET |
| Engagement signal | Saves and reposts far outweigh likes — a reference-value thread people bookmark |

**The extracted structure (topic stripped):** *Bold-claim hook with a credibility number,
then a numbered list of common mistakes in the audience's domain each paired with a fix,
then a reward (saveable resource) and a soft offer.* Hook type: bold claim + listicle
promise. Format: thread (X) / carousel (Instagram). Emotional driver: relief plus utility.
Save trigger: reference value. Lever family: utility.

**Fit check.** The founder has earned authority (they read store analytics daily), the
topic sits squarely on buyer pain (operators fear they are leaving money on the table),
and product adjacency is direct (the tool surfaces exactly these mistakes). All three
filters clear. The save trigger matches the goal metric.

**Three niche post ideas, structure preserved, content rebuilt:**

1. **X thread.** Hook: "I reviewed 30 Shopify stores' analytics last month. Here are the
   7 numbers most owners track wrong:" Body: one mis-tracked metric per tweet (vanity
   sessions over revenue-per-visitor, last-click over assisted, gross over contribution
   margin), each with the one-line fix. Close: a free "7 numbers that actually matter"
   checklist plus a soft mention of the tool. Native to X: text-forward, hook in the
   first line, value in the post.

2. **Instagram carousel.** Cover slide (the hook): "7 store metrics you're reading wrong
   (and what to track instead)." Slides 2–8: one mistake-and-fix per slide, the contrast
   (wrong vs. right) carried visually. Last slide: the reward (save this) and the CTA.
   Native to Instagram: the carousel is the save engine, the cover slide does the
   stopping, the contrast layout fits the visual room.

3. **Instagram Reel.** Hook (first second, spoken and captioned): "If you only check
   revenue, you're flying blind — here are 3 numbers that actually predict your next
   month." Body: three fast mistake-to-fix beats to camera, captions on, vertical. Close:
   "Saved this? The full 7 are in my bio." Native to Reels: first-second hook, sound-off
   captions, the widest reach to non-followers.

Each idea reuses the *bold-claim-number, then numbered mistakes-with-fixes, then saveable
reward, then soft offer* skeleton, and each replaces every piece of the borrowed content
with the founder's domain, the buyer's pain, and the founder's voice. Each names a
platform, a format, and a hook line. None is posted: the three drafts go to the user for
approval before any of them touches a live account.
