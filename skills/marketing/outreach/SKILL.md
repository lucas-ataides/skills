Every outward message obeys one principle: earn trust, make one clear ask, never over-promise. A cold email earns a reply when a stranger recognizes their own problem and one easy next step. A sales asset closes when the value is obvious, the proof is credible, and the live objection is already answered. A customer message holds trust when it names the situation plainly, owns what went wrong, and states what happens next. The discipline is the same across all three; the structure changes with the audience.

This skill **drafts** only. Sending anything — an email, a public post, a status-page update, an in-app banner, a sequence handed to an automation tool — is an external mutation against real recipients, a sender's domain reputation, or what every customer sees, and it cannot be taken back. So this skill never sends and never schedules a send: the user reviews every message and the user sends it. Hand the finished draft back and stop.

Route the request to one of three jobs first, then follow that job's depth:

- **Cold outbound** to strangers — see [the cold-email reference](references/cold-email.md): anatomy, personalization tiers, deliverability and spam, the one-CTA rule, follow-up cadence, CAN-SPAM and GDPR, a worked sequence.
- **Sales assets** that close a known deal — see [the sales-enablement reference](references/sales-enablement.md): the asset taxonomy, value-led drafting, proof structures, the acknowledge→reframe→evidence→confirm objection framework, battlecards, ROI framing, a worked objection.
- **Customer and PR messages** — see [the customer-comms reference](references/customer-comms.md): message types, tone calibration, the hard-news five-beat structure, incident and crisis basics, saying sorry well, avoiding legalese and over-promising, a worked outage apology.

## Steps

1. **Route the request and name the one job.** Pick the lane — cold outbound, sales asset, or customer/PR message — and write one sentence stating the single job this piece must do for its reader. Done when the lane is named and the job is one sentence, with no second job blended in.

2. **Gather what the lane requires.** Pull the inputs that lane demands from the matching reference: a cold email needs a named persona plus a named trigger; a sales asset needs the deal stage plus the one blocker; a customer message needs the facts the message type requires (for an incident: what happened, impact, status, fix, prevention). Done when every required input is recorded or marked as a gap to confirm with the user, with no invented detail.

3. **Draft to the lane's structure, reader-first, one ask.** Write to the shape the matching reference specifies — the six-part cold email, the value-led asset opening on the buyer's outcome, or the hard-news beats leading with the news — keeping the reader as the subject and carrying exactly one call to action or next step. Done when the draft follows its named structure and contains exactly one ask.

4. **Attach proof and calibrate tone.** Bind each value claim to evidence (a named result, a metric, a quote, a third-party signal) and set the register the situation demands (researched specificity for cold outbound, outcome-led credibility for a sales asset, the tone-calibration register for a customer message). Done when no value claim stands without evidence beside it and the register matches the news the reader is about to receive.

5. **Strip evasions, spam triggers, and over-promises.** Cut the pitch-slap and the all-about-us tilt, the spam signals (more than one link, an attachment, banned spam words, more than one exclamation point, broken merge tags), the non-apology ("sorry if anyone was affected"), the blame-shift, the legalese, and every commitment the team cannot guarantee, per the red-flags list in the matching reference. Done when the matching reference's red-flags list is clean against the draft, recorded as a pass.

6. **Add the lane's compliance or follow-through floor.** For cold outbound, build the follow-up sequence and verify the CAN-SPAM and GDPR floor (real sender identity, non-deceptive subject, a working opt-out in every touch, a physical postal address); for a sales asset, add the one-line "use this when" note; for a customer message, name the channel the draft is written for. Done when the lane's floor item is present and recorded.

7. **Hand off for approval.** Return the finished draft, label its intended channel, and state that sending is the user's call. Done when the draft is delivered with its channel labeled and nothing has been sent.

## Notes

This skill stops at a reviewed draft. Handing a message to a sending tool, an email API, a status page, or an automation platform is the user's decision and the user's action. The grounding doctrine is [the foundation determinism rules](../../meta/foundation/SKILL.md); the review depth bar for judging a finished draft is the tech-lead standard in [the review lenses](../../engineering/code-review/references/review-lenses.md).
